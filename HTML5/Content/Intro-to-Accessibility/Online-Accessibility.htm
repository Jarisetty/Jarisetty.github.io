Online Accessibility

Online Accessibility Standards

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) has developed a set of guidelines for web accessibility, called Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). While these standards aren't legally binding, many countries have adopted them and require certain websites to use them. These standards are analogous to ADA requirements, which mandate government and public businesses to follow accessibility laws.

Adherence to WCAG standards comes in three levels: A, AA, and AAA. Level A is the lowest and covers the bare minimum of accessibility by removing basic major obstacles for a wide range of users. Level AA is the standard for most websites. It provides a higher level of accessibility by removing obstacles and making websites work for most devices and assistive technologies. Level AAA is the highest level of adherence and follows most or all WCAG guidelines to provide the most complex web accessibility.

Designing for Online Accessibility

WCAG standards use four principles to structure web accessibility. These principles are called POUR: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust. Perceivable means that the webpage content must be understandable to all users. Operable means that the navigation and components of the page must be useable for all users. Understandable means that users must be able to understand the information and meaning of the webpage as well as how to operate the webpage. Robust means that the webpage remains usable on a wide range of devices with a wide range of assistive technologies.
