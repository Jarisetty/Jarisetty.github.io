Disability and Accessibility

Disability Perspectives

"Disability" is a legal term that is technically defined as a "physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activity" (ADA FAQ). This definition is useful for medical and legal purposes so that officials can make standardized decisions. But this perspective (known as the "medical model" of disability) treats disabilities as personal conditions that damage an individual. Using this model in daily life often results in trying to change disabled individuals to fit the world around them.

The "social model" of disability emphasizes a mismatch between a person and their environment. This perspective considers circumstances to be disabling, which relieves the burden of adaptation from the individual. Instead, it holds systems responsible for making environments suitable for a wide range of abilities.

Disability is not one-size-fits-all. One accommodation--like a wheelchair ramp--may not suit every impairment--such as a blind person trying to find their destination. Additionally, some cases of disability are not permanent. Some impairments are temporary or situational. A person with an ear infection or in a loud environment may need the same audio accessibility as a deaf person.

Accessibility Perspectives

"Accessibility" is the degree to which something is usable by a diverse range of people. "Web accessibility" is the degree to which a website is usable by a range of people. Keeping these ideas in mind is key to creating products that meet design standards.

"Accessible design" is a design goal which meets the needs of disabled users. This is important, but often it ends up being an afterthought that doesn't properly meet those needs. A better way is to incorporate accessibility into the design method, so that the product meets the needs of a diverse population. This is called "universal design".
