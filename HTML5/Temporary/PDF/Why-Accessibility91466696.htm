Why Accessibility Matters

The best place to start with accessibility standards is in the design stage. By incorporating accessibility principles from the very start, designers and constructors avoid treating those principles as last-minute add-ons. Respecting users of all abilities starts by treating accessibility with respect and care. Making adaptive products that function with wide variation in ability is part of creating better products that reach a wider population. Disregarding accessibility standards because they don't seem immediately beneficial or seem too difficult to design is a disservice to the designer and the users.

Accessibility features do not exclude people without impairments and can make the product experience better for everyone. Accessible features can also improve commercial appeal because they increase the number of people who can use the product. As web reliance increases, it is important for designers to refrain from designing for an "ideal user" who has no vision, hearing, mobility, or cognitive impairments. Making accessibility a core part of design prevents us from creating products that cut out actual users and limit the product's usability. Furthermore, focusing on what is universally important or necessary for use, instead of just ticking "accessibility" in the final design phase, helps improve the user experience for everyone.
